01
Entel
Telecoms · Peru
🥇 Gold Effie — Telecoms
🥉 Bronze Effie — Product
Challenge

"How do you launch a product no one in the category had ever talked about?"

Entel needed to introduce additional phone lines and drive group migration to postpaid — a product the entire telecom category had ignored. The conventional approach was a promotional SMS that looked like spam. We needed something that felt human.

Drawing on social identity theory and the psychology of collaborative consumption, we reframed additional lines not as a product feature but as an act of sharing. The insight: Peruvians are among the most savings-oriented people in the region, and sharing to save is deeply culturally resonant. The campaign "Cuando compartimos, ahorramos" (When we share, we save) positioned Entel alongside Netflix and Spotify as a brand built on the logic of sharing — not selling.

02
RIMAC
Insurance · Peru
Source: Ipsos
Challenge

"Reaching 3.7 million prospective customers who had considered insurance — but never bought it."

RIMAC's new strategic positioning required attracting a segment fundamentally disengaged from the insurance category. Research showed 83% had considered buying insurance, yet 70% couldn't find one that fit their needs, 69% found them too expensive, and 40% didn't know who to contact.

We built a three-track research and innovation programme: an audience deep dive (500+ surveys and 20 in-depth interviews to construct new archetypes), a comms workshop across journey, product, and communications tracks, and a freemium pilot testing new voice, tone, and product formats directly with target consumers. The result was a more empathetic product design process grounded in real behavioural barriers — validated by Top 2 Box scores from Ipsos brand testing.

03
Inka Chips
FMCG · Peru
Challenge

"Building a brand pyramid and digital strategy from the ground up for an iconic Peruvian snack brand."

Inka Chips needed to formalise its brand identity and translate it into a coherent digital presence that resonated with its core audiences. Starting with a desk research phase synthesising both quantitative and qualitative studies the brand had conducted, alongside market trend analysis, we identified a brand territory rooted in cultural pride and taste heritage.

The territory "Demanding taste by heritage" gave the brand a platform that felt authentic and differentiated. From this foundation, we developed four brand battles — competitive positioning frameworks — and built a digital strategy that mapped the brand's communication style, tone, and content approach to the specific passion points of its target audiences. The result was a brand architecture the marketing team could execute consistently across all channels.

4 Brand battle frameworks
1 Brand pyramid + full digital strategy
04
Culture Lab
FMCG Innovation · Alicorp
Clients: Casino, BCP, Falabella
Challenge

"Building an innovation unit from scratch — and turning ephemeral trends into lasting cultural moments."

Casino, Alicorp's biscuit brand, needed to move beyond campaign thinking and connect with new audience tribes it had never reached. I founded and led Culture Lab, the agency's first internal innovation unit, designing a proprietary methodology combining design thinking and Agile governance that could be adapted across different clients and objectives.

For Casino specifically, this meant three interconnected workstreams: a governance and process architecture giving the brand a system to respond to trends in real time; a route-to-market innovation programme including tribe immersions and pilot quantification; and a cultural moments strategy turning small trends into scalable activations. Executions included Más Gamers Festival co-immersion, a Wonka x Casino Lab LVL Party, and Barbieheimer — all translating audience subcultures into commercially relevant brand moments. Culture Lab became one of the agency's most important projects, subsequently used with BCP and Falabella.

3 Major brand clients
1st Innovation unit at agency

05
Treble.ai
SaaS · B2B · Colombia
Challenge

"A strong product with no clear positioning — and a LatAm market full of competitors."

Treble.ai had built a compelling B2B conversational AI platform but lacked the strategic clarity to differentiate it in a crowded regional market. They needed to articulate who they were, who they were for, and why it mattered — before they could sell it effectively.

I designed and delivered a three-day brand strategy workshop in Bogotá with Treble's senior leadership team. The process combined buyer persona development, pain-point mapping, and competitive positioning to identify the key insight driving the brand: that understanding the emotional and functional needs of the buyer persona — not just the product specs — was the key to conversion. The workshop output was a complete brand pyramid, consolidated positioning, and a go-to-market strategy with differentiated messaging for each principal customer segment.

02 — About

I grew up in Peru, where complexity is not abstract — electoral instability, economic precarity, and institutional distrust are the context in which people make daily decisions. That background shaped how I approach strategy: curiosity first, frameworks second.

After five years leading innovation and strategy within the WPP network across Latin America, I moved to London to formalise that instinct with rigorous academic training. At LSE I work as a Research Assistant in the Behavioural Lab, running eye-tracking experiments on sustainable consumer behaviour, and I'm completing a dissertation on electoral uncertainty as a driver of present bias in financial decision-making in Peru.

I work across English and Spanish, and across disciplines — from data analysts and UX designers to economists and creative directors. The common thread is always the same: understanding why people do what they do, and designing solutions that work with that understanding rather than against it.
